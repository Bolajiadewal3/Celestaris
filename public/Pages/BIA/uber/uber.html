2.2 Increasing Time Estimation Accuracy

Uber’s estimated arrival-times are generated utilising predictive analytics, with accepted error margins (Hu et al., 2022a). Uber utilises terrain maps and the Global Navigation Satellite System [GNSS ] (Iland, 2018). Certain functions are employed to minimise prediction errors (i.e. shadow mapping allows for accuracy in low-confidence areas). While this is beneficial; the model currently requires downtime to test, limiting real-time accuracy (Hu et al., 2022b). A method should allow for continuous, incrementaltraining instead (Hu et al., 2022b).

Figure 5: Alex’s User Story

2.3 Reducing chance of driver loops

Drivers looping could be reduced by increased rider/driver contact time, and extreme options:

If a driver takes too long to arrive at their destination, and has been static for a while, a notification could allow them to re-offer the job to nearby ubers. The driver could receive a percentage compensation, or still complete the job if they begin to move again – however it a car that is on a less congested route could complete the job in a more timely fashion: keeping uber customer-centric.

In addition, a slight delay to movement animation on the rider side application is recommended. The map view is prone to car icon jumps in areas when shadow mapping is applied – instead of just GNSS (Shekhar & Sankaravadivel, 2019). Adding a subtle delay would gift more time to allow location smoothing (Shekhar & Sankaravadivel, 2019).

2.4 Increasing the rider/contract time

To increase contact time between operators: drivers must either text or call riders upon arrival at least once. If not, it could negatively affect their score. Additionally, a notification should tell the rider to message their driver, not just meet them.

Figure 5: Alex’s User Story

2.5 Decreasing steps walked by riders

Currently, map-matching calculates accurate spots for drivers (Shekhar & Sankaravadivel, 2019). Other methods are more accurate but computationally intense (Shekhar & Sankaravadivel, 2019). An example is dead reckoning which allows for smoothing between poor GPS points utilising mathematical equations (Shekhar & Sankaravadivel, 2019). To conglomerate these results: Beacon conveys information to differing phones by deciding on formulae in accordance with the device in use (Shekhar & Sankaravadivel, 2019).

Some devices do not convey certain parameters for shadow matching – as they have rules governing information sharing. For instance, iPhones do not automatically send information, thus other, costly methods must be expended [i.e. Bluetooth connections] (Shekhar & Sankaravadivel, 2019).

iPhones thus need to be better leveraged technologically to extract their GPS information, to allow for more time to correct errors and display information to the user.

3.0 Pickup automation and experience improvement through artifical intelligence & machine learning

3.1 The business problem

How does one provide an effortless pickup experience for everyone, everywhere, everytime

The basic need offered by Uber is effortless transit (Cooper, 2014; Sawhney et al., 2019): This benefits the customer as well as the riders - who gain an income avenue; while external stakeholders gain profits.

The desired objective is provision of the perfect pickup (MBEC, 2022; Sawhney et al., 2019). Steps towards combatting the business problem have already been initiated, with a 2016 paradigm-shift towards destination-first customer autonomy (Sawhney et al., 2019). In addition, two ML platforms have been employed before: Horovod and Michelangelo – but are open to improvement (Shao & Islam, 2021a). Uber actively embraces Artificial Intelligence, so the goals of this solution are in alignment with the company’s philosophy. In conjunction, the benefits of such decision support will be monetary in value. (Dean, 2014):

Figure 5: Alex’s User Story

Uber’s true problem is the need to enhance methods for relationship management between stakeholders. The principle of choice henceforth is a model that can provide said enhancement consistently (Turban et al., 2015). In solving the surface and deep-level business problems; Uber could adopt the Agile methodology for continuous improvement upon the ML model that is to be described (Project Management Institute, 2017; Harirajan, 2022).

3.2 Viability of Machine Learning

Machine learning, which has already been employed, is only of use - in this scenario - to Uber if other methods of prediction fail (Dean, 2014). Simple mathematical equations are not suitable for the number of factors that Uber stores. This data is largely unstructured, stored in a format that is difficult in understanding for humans, crafting a perfect opportunity for ML to be employed (Lee & Obermeyer, 2017).

Currently, Uber utilises a metric to model the quality of their data storage – CAP (Zhong & Cheng, 2021):

Cost-Efficiency X Accuracy X Performance = Constant

The underperformer in the model currently is performance, with the cost of collecting data being minimised already by a number of size reduction techniques such as– data storage on the Apache® Hadoop® File System (HDFS) space , in Apache Hive TM tables (Shao & Islam 2021b).

Machine learning is necessary due to the sheer amount of data that Uber receives. It has 134,000 HDDs allocated to information storage, with the average size being 4TB (Shao & Islam, 2021c).

ML is therefore a viable option: there is a well-defined problem to be solved by such algorithms; there is an abundance of continuously updated data; said data is relevant to the problem and cleanable; the results of data analysis would directly impact the problem and; it is clear that the volume of data prevents problem solution through traditional means (Dean, 2014).

A structured decision problem can thus be presented:

How can profit be maximised, when profit directly correlates to pickup quality

3.3 Data Gathering

Uber’s data storage initially utilised only Online transaction processing databases [OLTP] to handle their data requirement (Reza, 2018). This provided fast latency for TBs of data. In 2016, this was replaced with a data-warehouse. Difficulty in its use stemmed from a lack of rule enforcement on JSON entries from data sources (Reza, 2018).

Following this, Hadoop adoption facilitated data-lake creation: to store standardised Parquet data (Reza, 2018). Since, Hudi has been added to abstract and allow querying based on time metrics (Reza, 2018). This data-lake is a colossal information store, combining features of both operational databases and data-warehouses via a Kimball approach (Reza, 2018; Sharma, 2023a; Govindarajan, 2023):

3.4 Data Pre-processing

Both CRISP-DM and SEMMA are viable methods for modelling Uber’s real-time data (Turban et al., 2015). However, CRISP-DM holds an assumption that the business understanding of the model is not yet identified (Turban et al., 2015). Thus, SEMMA will be utilised.

Data sanitation is paramount to the success of the ML model. Schemaless can extract information on trips a sample size can be taken (Thomsen, 2016). The queries present information in formats that are acceptable, thus the biggest challenge is the data sanitation (Hermann, 2017). Data must be inspected and incomplete values must be rectified before training occurs (Sharma, 2023a; Turban et al., 2015).

Artificial sampling of missing values is not viable as the possible feature values are impossible to predict (Sharma, 2023b; Acaps, 2016). For instance, the number of times a driver looped around a rendezvous cannot be merely chosen from a selection (Iland et al., 2018).

Instead, the arithmetic mean of a feature will be selected to fill in missing values (Sharma, 2023b; Acaps, 2016). For example, to predict a driver’s ETA for their 11 th job, the previous 10 jobs ETAs to ATA times could be scrutinised.

Outlier detection to prevent severe skew can be performed utilising the inter-quartile method (Sharma, 2023b; Yang, 2019). While the standard deviate method could apply to some features, it is safer to assume the central limit theorem due to the size of the dataset (LaMorte, 2016; Yang, 2019).

Uber employs offline and online data in Michelangelo (Hermann, 2017). The data is fed into the model through a sub-set of the Scala programming language (Hermann, 2017). Uber additionally utilises principal component analysis to find outliers in data– before it arrives at the ML model (Li et al., 2020).

A linear programming model could be formulated to better express the objective of the ML model:

maximise = 0.15R – 0.3T – 0.25S - 0.2L – 0.1P

This would apply if the selected metric was the same as earlier. A wider viewpoint on the same problem is: the ML should identify features that contribute to an increase in the number of high-quality rides completed per time period (Sawhney, 2019).

3.5 Machine Learning Algorithms

The crux of the business problem relies on factors that impact high-quality completed rides per unit time. With this single regression or classification (by setting a threshold) algorithms would be suitable (Chikohora, 2014).

A relationship must be discerned between the features and the pickup experience. This warrants a linear regression model, but this may not be effective enough due to the size of the dataset (Chikohora, 2014). As well as this, the least squared error may prove a lack of strong correlation (Sharma, 2023c). Thus, supervised learning model must assist.

To be specific: supervised decision trees could discern which features have the strongest impact on the classification of an experience by an operator (Sharma, 2023c). It could then be revealed that a number of factors have a larger impact than assumed.

In addition, unsupervised learning can be employed. Apriori would not accurately represent the nature of the uber experience (Sharma, 2023d; Xiong, 2005). Most its conclusions would be on causally paired values (Sharma, 2023d; Xiong, 2005). Market basket analysis may identify a strong correlation between cancellation rate and rider/driver contact rate in the United States; however the cancellation rate could be due to the low contact rate – as opposed to coinciding with it.

In order to model such scenarios – unsupervised neural networks could be trained (Sharma, 2023e). They could be utilised to independently verify the results generated by the decision trees (Sharma, 2023e).

If such data is unavailable, then the network could be used to determine whether or not a certain threshold of completed jobs per unit time occurs, conveying a positive pickup experience (Sharma, 2023e).

Note: numerical values could be normalised in the neural network to save computational time and improve accuracy (Sharma, 2023e; Stöttner, 2019).

3.6 Features & Model improvement

To map the strength of the various features to Uber’s success, the linear regression model could once again be utilised. If the predicted variable is that of average trips completed per unit time ornumber of greatpieces of user feedback: after receiving an output, the regression coefficients of the various predictor variables could be plotted to instantly view which has the highest impact (Sharma, 2023c).

With this information, it would be easier to craft a quality metric, as the features that have the largest impact would be selected and their regression coefficients would their impact on pickup quality (Sharma, 2023c).

Note: the regression coefficients do not correlate to the overall impact if only the most impactful portion is selected! the regression model would need to be rebuilt with only said features present (Sharma, 2023c).

Once these features are discerned, analysis could take place. Due to the real-time nature of Uber, the online segment used in Michelangelo’s data-stores could be consistently used to test the model (Hermann, 2017).

As is already done by Uber, a portion of the predictions will be withheld and if the classification accuracy – utilising ROC curves - models; or the regression coefficients drastically change in comparison to the real-time data – a more recent portion of data could be retrieved to retrain the ML model (Sharma, 2023c; Sharma, 2023e; Hermann, 2017).

3.7 User Experience

If the ML model discerns that a certain driver has delivered poor quality rides for a set duration of time, it could reduce the number of jobs or the rating of riders that appear for a portion of time.

Based on the outputs from the ML model, clear communication could be conveyed to both riders and drivers on the states of their pickups. If the model predicts that the time to arrive has changed to be far longer than previously anticipated, then riders could be informed via a message.

Post journey – if the driver is associated with a high quality pickup experience value, they could be offered far more jobs for the rest of the day, while a customer with a similar value may be targeted for promotions and deals.
